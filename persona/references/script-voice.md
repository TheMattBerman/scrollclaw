# Script Voice System

How to write UGC scripts that sound like a person talking to their phone.

## The specificity test

Before writing: who exactly is watching and what is going through their head right now?

Not "millennials interested in fitness" but "28-year-old women who have tried three different workout programs in the last year, feel overwhelmed by conflicting nutrition advice, and are skeptical of anything promising fast results because they've been burned before."

That level of specificity changes everything about how you write. Use the persona research from `persona-research.md` to get there.

## The rule

If you can imagine a copywriter writing it, rewrite it.

UGC scripts should sound like the creator just hit record and started talking. Not polished. Not structured. Not hitting beats.

## Voice mechanics

### Filler words
Use them. Real people say "like," "um," "so," "honestly," "okay so," "I don't know," "whatever." Not in every sentence — sprinkle naturally.

### Restarts
People start sentences and restart them. "I think — actually no, what I mean is..." This signals real thought in progress.

### Mid-thought entry
Don't start at the beginning. Start in the middle. "okay so I've had this for like two weeks now and—" is better than "Hi everyone, today I'm going to review..."

### Hedging
Real people hedge their opinions. "I think it's working? Like my skin definitely looks different but I don't want to jinx it." Not: "The results have been incredible."

### Specificity over superlatives
"This lasted through my whole shift and I work 10-hour days" beats "This has amazing lasting power." Specific moments > broad claims.

### Mild negative honesty
Including one genuine criticism makes everything else believable. "The packaging is kind of ugly honestly but I don't care because look at this." Real people nitpick even things they love.

### Emotional texture
Not just "I like this product." Real reactions: annoyed it works ("I'm mad about how good this is"), reluctant admission ("fine. it's good. I hate that they were right"), genuine surprise ("wait what"), skepticism that converted ("I thought this was going to be bullshit").

## Script register levels

### Level 1: Stream of consciousness
Lowest polish. Best for TikTok, casual product reactions.

Example:
```
okay so. someone in my comments kept telling me to try this and I was like no because I've tried literally everything and nothing works. but I caved. it's been like 12 days? and um. look. [shows result] I'm annoyed. I'm actually annoyed because why does this work and the $80 one didn't.
```

### Level 2: Casual direct
Slightly more structured but still conversational. Good for reviews, talking heads.

Example:
```
alright so I've been using this for about three weeks. here's the thing — I almost returned it after day one because the texture was weird. like really weird. but I stuck with it and by week two I started noticing my [specific result]. I'm not saying it's magic. I'm saying it's the only thing that's actually done something.
```

### Level 3: Informed casual
Has authority but doesn't lecture. Good for expert-position creators, comparison content.

Example:
```
so I've tested probably 30 of these at this point. most of them are the same formula repackaged. this one's actually different and I'll tell you why — [specific ingredient/feature]. that matters because [specific reason from experience]. I'm not sponsored, I bought this. take that for what it's worth.
```

## The read-aloud test

Read the script out loud. Ask yourself: have you ever heard anyone actually speak this way in real life? Real people ramble. Real people correct themselves mid-sentence. Real people use filler words and pause at weird moments. Real people don't speak in perfectly structured sentences with a hook, three supporting points, and a clean call to action.

If the script sounds like a script, rewrite it until it sounds like talking.

## What to never write

- "Hey guys!" or any greeting that signals content creation
- "In this video I'm going to..." — just start
- "Make sure to like and subscribe" — in UGC this is death
- "I partnered with [brand]" unless it's a genuine sponsorship disclosure
- Perfect grammar throughout — mix in fragments
- Three-point structures — real rambling doesn't come in threes
- Clean endings — trail off, get distracted, or just stop
- Perfectly structured hook → three points → CTA — this is the skeleton of every bad AI script

## Script template

```
[HOOK — mid-thought entry, 1-2 sentences, creates curiosity or states a strong take]

[BODY — the actual content. Show the thing, use the thing, react to the thing. Include at least one genuine hesitation or criticism. Specific moments > broad claims.]

[CLOSE — one line. Not a CTA unless it's throwaway casual. "yeah so. that's where I'm at with this." or just stop talking.]
```

The template is loose on purpose. The worst UGC scripts are the ones that follow a rigid structure.

## Adapting to voice personality

Each creator profile has a voice personality section. Use it.

A skeptical creator hedges more and includes more negatives before admitting something works.
An enthusiastic creator gets excited faster but still includes real moments.
A deadpan creator understates everything — "it's fine. it works. whatever."

The script register should match the creator, not the brand's preferred tone. That's what makes it feel real.
